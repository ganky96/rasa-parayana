If you walk into a temple with many murtis, or hear hymns naming Indra, Agni, Sarasvatī, and Śiva in the same breath, it is easy to answer "yes." Popular English usage often files anything with "many gods" under polytheism. For Hindus, the more interesting question is what those many names *do* in practice, and whether they name separate ultimate origins or many faces of one reality.

Classical Hindu texts do not tidy themselves into the categories that European religious studies inherited from Abrahamic polemics. The *Ṛgveda* already presses toward unity: "Truth is one; the wise express it in many ways" (often quoted from *Ṛgveda* 1.164.46 in paraphrase). Philosophical schools disagree sharply on details, yet you will find sustained arguments that the apparent multiplicity of devas is nested within a single *Brahman* (the expansive principle of being–consciousness), or that Īśvara is one while the world's plurality is *māyā* or *śakti*—real in a dependent sense, not a second absolute.

In lived Hinduism, a village might worship a gramadevatā, a family observes kuladevatā, and a pilgrim bows at a pan-Indian shrine. That looks like polytheism from the outside. From the inside, many Hindus will say these are *forms* (*rūpa*) or *manifestations* through which the same divine can be approached, not a council of rival sovereigns. Others, influenced by bhakti or Vedānta, will speak in frankly monotheistic language about one supreme deity "with" many subordinate powers—language that maps poorly onto Greek Olympus.

So is Hinduism polytheistic? Sometimes, when the question means only "Are there many divine names and icons?" If the question means "Does Hindu thought posit many independent, ultimate creators in permanent competition?" the mainstream answer across major darśanas is *no*. The tradition is better described as **henotheistic**, **monistic**, or **polymorphic monotheism**—labels are imperfect. What matters is the refusal to flatten either the richness of popular devotion or the unity claims of śruti and Vedānta. Hinduism is not a single creed; it is a family of practices and philosophies. On this question, honest answers begin with "It depends what you mean by god—and who is asking."
